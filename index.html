Reflections

8 Things 18 Years in Governance Taught Me

A reflective post built from real BA/governance experience — the kind that resonates because it's specific, not generic.

Before you publish: this is a structural draft, not your story. Swap each point below for a real moment from your own career — the RCSA finding that changed how you documented things, the audit that taught you something the hard way, the conversation that shifted your approach to stakeholders. Specificity is what makes this post land.
01

Controls fail on people, not paper.

The best-designed control framework still breaks down if the person executing it doesn't understand why it matters. Documentation alone never closed a gap — training and trust did.

02

Audit readiness is a daily habit, not a scramble.

Teams that update evidence continuously walk into audits calm. Teams that backfill in the final week never quite catch up — and it shows.

03

The quietest stakeholder in the room is often the riskiest to ignore.

The person who never pushes back isn't always aligned — sometimes they're disengaged, and disengagement is where scope gaps hide.

04

Governance work is invisible until it isn't.

Nobody notices the incident that didn't happen because the control worked. Learning to document and claim that value was its own skill.

05

PII exposure hides in the most mundane places.

Not in the systems everyone's watching — in the shared drive nobody's reviewed since 2019. Reduction work is mostly about looking where no one else is looking.

06

A RACI conversation prevents more conflict than any escalation ever resolves.

Most "difficult stakeholders" are really just unclear ownership wearing a person's face.

07

Longevity is a credibility asset — use it deliberately.

Eighteen years across institutions means pattern recognition new analysts don't have yet. Naming that expertise, rather than downplaying it, is part of leading.

08

The promotion case writes itself when the documentation habit is already there.

If you're tracking contributions as you go, review season is a compilation exercise, not a scramble to remember what you did in March.

BA Fundamentals

Root Cause Analysis: Fishbone + Pareto in Practice

Two of the most reliable tools for cutting through symptom-chasing and finding the actual source of a recurring problem — used together, they take you from "what's causing this" to "what's worth fixing first."

Example problem: Recurring approval delays are slowing down a monthly reporting cycle. Here's how the categories break down:

👥 People
  • Unclear approval ownership
  • Competing priorities
  • No backup approver
⚙️ Process
  • Too many approval layers
  • No defined SLA
  • Inconsistent criteria
💻 Technology
  • Clunky approval system
  • Poor notifications
  • No mobile access
📋 Governance
  • No approval policy
  • No escalation path
  • SLA not enforced

Then Apply the 80/20 Rule

Once causes are mapped, resist the urge to fix everything at once. In most recurring-problem analyses, roughly three causes account for the majority of incidents. Rank causes by frequency, plot the cumulative impact, and focus remediation on whichever handful cross the 80% threshold first. Fixing the trivial many feels productive — fixing the vital few is what actually moves the metric.

My Coaching Philosophy

Delivering successful projects isn't just about documenting requirements; it's about understanding the people behind them. I believe that the best professionals possess a dual-threat capability: sharp technical acumen and deep emotional intelligence.

Whether you are a Business Analyst looking to master Agile delivery and documentation, or a leader striving to build psychological safety and conduct high-impact 1-on-1s, my coaching provides the exact frameworks you need to turn data into decisions and conversations into growth.

Coaching Tracks

Track A: Business Analysis Mastery

Navigate the modern BA landscape with confidence, from foundational techniques to Agile ceremonies.

  • Agile Delivery: Master the art of writing testable User Stories, defining Acceptance Criteria, and leading Backlog Refinements.
  • Documentation Clarity: Understand exactly when and how to use a BRD (the "What"), an FRD (the "How"), and an SRS (the Blueprint).
  • Core Techniques: Apply the 10 essential BA tools, including Root Cause Analysis (5 Whys), Process Mapping, and MoSCoW Prioritization.
  • Stakeholder Alignment: Prevent bottlenecks by mastering the RACI Matrix (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed).

Track B: Emotional Intelligence (EQ) & Leadership

Develop the soft skills necessary to lead with empathy, calm the busy mind, and foster genuine connection.

  • Empathetic Communication: Transition from "Listening to Fix" (rushing to solve) to "Listening to Connect" (creating safety and stillness).
  • Overcoming the Busy Mind: Identify and dismantle cognitive traps like "The Fortune Teller" and "The Judge" to build professional resilience.
  • The EQ Coach Approach: Structure powerful 1-on-1s using the 3-step Engage, Activate, and Reflect framework.
  • The Wheel of Coaching: Guide your team from defining their current state to exploring open questions and practicing actionable next steps.
